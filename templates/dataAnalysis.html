Data Analysis


This data analysis was done using a Kaggle movies dataset called movies_metadata.csv, click here to access the dataset

Fig 1: Number of High Grossing Movies (>10M USD) Per Release Year


The number of high grossing movies released per year has increased a lot since 1950. There was a steady climb until the mid 1970's then the gradient increased from the mid-1970s to around 2000. Then the gradient increased again, with the Y value doubling and going from just over 60 to 120 in just over a 10 year period.

There are a number of factors behind this. Inflation is one - it was much harder to make a movie with a profit of more than 10M USD when the USD was worth a lot more. Another is that more blockbusters are being made. Often these are a part of franchises, with the top 10 highest profit movies all being from big-budget franchises (at the time the dataset goes up to) aside from Avatar, Titanic and Frozen (Avatar and Frozen have subsequently had sequels). The rest of the top 10 includes movies from Star Wars, the MCU, Harry Potter and Jurassic World. Big budget franchises spend lots of money on marketing, spend lots of getting the biggest stars and best special effects, and they are a known product, so lots of people go to see them at the box office. This is important with the advent of streaming, as the majority of a movie's revenue will come from the box office compared to 15 years ago and prior when a mid budget movie could make a lot through DVD sales and through renting services such as Blockbuster.

Fig 2: Number of High Grossing Movies in Franchise Per Release Year

This is backed up by the data as seen in Fig 2. The number of movies in a franchise (defined in the dataset as more than 1 movie with a similar title and that share the same fictional universe) was below 10 until the mid 1970's, then rose steadily and plateaud until around 2000 when it jumped to 30-40 per year. This jump happens at the same point the gradient for high grossing movies sharply increases, suggesting a correlation. This can be seen more evidently if we include only the highest grossing films (over 1BN).

Fig 3: Number of High Grossing Movies (>1BN USD) in Franchise Per Release Year

The number of high grossing films in franchises is close or matching the number of high grossing films total, until around the year 2015 and the majority of high grossing films are still from franchises. So at the very top of the market, franchises are very dominant.

Fig 5: Polynomial Regression on Number of High Grossing Movies (>100M USD) Per Release Year

Linear and polynomial regression models are fitted to the data in Fig 4 and Fig 5. The linear regression has an R-Squared coefficient of 0.92 (to 2 DP) and the polynomial regression has an R-Squared coefficient of 0.97. The polynomial regression has the better fit suggesting that there is an X2 dependency on release year.
